From Survival to Strength


Posted: August 10, 2025



Fragile Beginnings

Growing up, I wasn’t a strong kid - in fact, I was extremely skinny.
Not by choice, but because most foods simply didn’t sit well with me. It felt as though I could lose weight just by reading the menu.
Looking back, I see now it was simply my body’s response to unknown allergies, anxious environments, and circumstances I didn’t have the language to understand at the time.

But one thing always brought me to life: netball.

Discovering Netball - My First Safe Space

When I joined the school netball team at eight years old, it became the first place I wasn’t living in comparison. The court felt like freedom - a space where I could be fully present and fully myself. I tried other sports, but nothing lit me up like netball.

Still, like in many South Asian homes, sport was rarely seen as important especially for girls. Academic success was the main focus, while creative or athletic passions were pushed aside. On top of that, many of us girls - myself included - carried the weight of caring for younger siblings and managing household chores, roles that were valued more than time spent on the court, in music, or in any pursuit beyond studies.

Success Met with Doubt

One day, something unexpected happened - our school announced the netball captains for the first time. I still remember the moment we were all called into the sports room. As we walked there, one of my teammates casually said, “If they’re going to appoint a captain, it’s going to be you.”  Her words lit something inside me. I hadn’t seen myself that way before, but hearing her say it made me feel seen - like maybe, in her eyes, I had earned the respect of my team.
And then it happened: both my sister and I were chosen as captains for our age groups. I still remember feeling so proud and so genuinely happy that I even finished every bit of food during the school break, which for me, was historic in itself.

But, when we shared the news at home, the response wasn’t quite what I expected.
Instead of celebrating, the focus shifted. Concerns were raised about how other parents might perceive the decision - that both my sister and I being chosen could be seen as favouritism rather than a coincidence, and so a call was made to our coach asking that I be removed from the position, while my sister remained.
Our coach, however, reassured that it was a team decision and that they truly believed I had rightfully earned the role.
But by then, the moment shifted. I sat there in silence - my excitement slowly fading. I couldn’t understand why because neither of my parents had the chance to ever come watch a match and so I felt like they had no say in our coach’s decisions. I was wondering how could anyone possibly think they had influenced this? Why would anyone believe my captaincy wasn’t deserving, when they had never been there to begin with?
Looking back now, I understand it differently. My parents were doing the best they could within the limits of what they knew - trying to prevent misunderstanding or criticism, not realising how much that decision would affect me. But at the time, I didn’t have the words for that perspective. I only felt the quiet sting of being unseen, especially by the people I wanted to celebrate with most. That was the first time I felt a flicker of what I would later come to recognise as imposter syndrome. I knew I had worked hard for it, yet part of me began to wonder if I really belonged.

***

A few years later, when I was named Best Shooter for our age group and my name appeared in the local newspaper, I was handed the paper by the school librarian who told me it featured my sister -
It seemed like a small mistake, but it pierced deeper than I expected. It reminded me of how easily I could be overlooked, even when I had earned my place.

***

Over time, I found myself drawn to athletics. I wanted to try middle-distance races and see what my body could do beyond netball.
On one occasion, while browsing sports equipment for training, my request for ankle weights was met with hesitation, and I was questioned "since when did you do athletics?" in front of the shop staff. I walked away feeling humiliated and that my interest in wanting to train wasn’t being taken seriously. Luckily, my partner gave me his spare ankle weights and dumbbells so I could train at home.
Not long after, I was appointed School Athletics Captain.

Even then, doubt from others continued to appear.
At our final school sports meet, I turned up for a relay I had trained for, only to discover my name had been replaced on the team list. It was the only event I had that day.
The sting was sharper than I could have imagined - not just from being left out, but from the silence of people I thought were my friends.
Having my partner and another friend there for support meant everything.
In that moment, I learned a very valuable lesson about friendship and about how easily people can misjudge your potential when they haven’t truly seen you.

Lessons in Resilience

These are just a few of the experiences that shaped me - moments that, at the time, felt discouraging, but ultimately became stepping stones in my growth. And I know I’m not alone in that. So many of us carry stories of being overlooked, misunderstood, or dismissed along the way.
With time and reflection, I’ve learned to see those experiences differently. They weren’t personal attacks, even though they once felt that way. They were simply reflections of how limited people’s perspectives can be - how easy it is for others to overlook potential they haven’t yet learned to recognise. These experiences taught me a valuable lesson:
You can’t control what people think of you, but you can control whether or not you believe it.

A New Chapter in Australia

Growing up, I often felt I had to shrink myself - emotionally, mentally, even physically - to keep the peace or to fit the mould that was expected of me. So when I moved to Australia in 2019, it felt like stepping into fresh air after years of holding my breath. I was ready for a new chapter - one that allowed me to explore who I truly was, separate from comparison or expectation.

Here, I discovered a freedom I had never known before - the freedom to redefine myself on my own terms.
One of the first steps in that process was walking into a gym for the first time - something I never imagined I’d do.
Growing up, I had internalised the belief that gyms were “for guys,” that strength wasn’t feminine, and that girls weren’t supposed to lift weights or build muscle.

But my partner gently challenged those ideas. With his patience and support, I found the courage to start. I weighed just 43kg at the time, and for the first time in my life, I wanted to get stronger - not to look a certain way, but to feel powerful in my body.

That shift marked the beginning of something much deeper than physical change. It was the start of reclaiming space - in my body, in my mind, and in my life.

Falling in Love with Movement

As I began to change how I nourished and moved my body, something beautiful unfolded - I started to gain healthy weight.
I built muscle. And for the first time ever, I saw my body as something to care for, honour, and celebrate, not something to fix or criticise.

Towards the end of 2020, gym training became a regular part of our lives.
I began to fall in love with movement - not just for the physical benefits, but for what it did for my mind.
It gave me structure when life felt uncertain, strength when I doubted myself, and clarity when my mind grew heavy.

In 2021, that relationship with movement expanded even more. I joined my university’s indoor netball team and even had the privilege of representing my university at the intervarsity games. That same year, I also ran my first 10km run - something I never thought my body would be capable of.

But just as I was beginning to find my rhythm, life had one more curveball in store.

The Setback That Changed Everything

During our 2022 netball finals, I sprinted to defend the ball when my right shoe slipped on the turf.
My right ankle twisted, and instinctively I threw my weight onto my left leg to stop the fall - but it slipped too. In that split second, my left knee hyperextended - and then it came - a sickening, unforgettable sound...
My left femur had snapped.

The pain was unlike anything I’ve ever experienced (on a scale of 1 to childbirth (so I’ve been told), it was somewhere in the ‘invent new swear words’ category 😭). In that moment, time seemed to slow, and all I could do was breathe through the shock of it.
Later scans confirmed what the doctors called a freak accident. My ankle and knee were completely fine, and my bone density was actually well above average at 1.3 g/cm² - even compared to male reference values, but the force of the fall had just been too much.

Looking back now, I can see that moment not as an ending, but as a turning point - one of those rare, life-altering pauses that redirect you toward something deeper.

Recovery & Support

I was hospitalised for a week, and in that time my partner never left my side. Each day, he showed up with the most delicious, nourishing, home-cooked meals – so much so that one of the nurses joked he was running a five-star catering service out of the hospital room 😂. Through the pain and the uncertainty, he kept my spirits up with goofy jokes, silly dances, and an unwavering presence that made even the hardest days feel softer.
I never once felt alone.

Rehabilitation that followed was slow and humbling. I had to relearn how to walk, move, and trust my body again. The smallest steps forward felt monumental. But in those moments, I learned something I hadn’t fully grasped before: movement is a privilege.
And from that moment on, I promised myself - I would never take it for granted again.

Bouncing Back Stronger

Since I had to ease off lower-body training, I decided to see it as an opportunity rather than a setback. I began focusing more on my upper body - and that’s when I discovered upper-body calisthenics, and really got into pull-ups, dips, and eventually weighted dips, push-ups, and even clap push-ups.
Watching my partner move with such strength and ease - performing muscle-ups and weighted pull-ups sparked my curiosity and motivation to train harder.

Over time, my body began to heal - and so did my relationship with it. The following year, I completed a 5km run, a milestone that a year ago felt impossible.
I started training with deeper intention - focusing on injury prevention, mobility, recovery, and long-term health rather than just progress.
My legs grew stronger, my body more resilient, and my mind steadier than ever before.

For the Girls Who Were Told They Couldn't


Growing up as a South Asian woman, strength wasn’t something I was taught to celebrate.
We were often encouraged to be soft, polite, and accommodating - to take up less space and prioritise harmony over truth.
For many of us, fitness was seen as unnecessary, unfeminine, or even inappropriate.

But I’ve learned that strength never belonged to one gender. It’s a quality that lives within every human being.
It’s not just in the muscles - it’s in the choices we make, the wounds we heal, and the respect we hold for ourselves.

That’s why I created this space.

A safe space for girls who have been overlooked, underestimated, or taught to dim their light.

This is a place to reclaim your power.
To reconnect with your body, calm your mind, open your heart, and awaken your soul.
To move not just for change on the outside, but for healing from within.

This journey isn’t about perfection or fitting into anyone else’s mould.
It’s about embracing who you are - whole, powerful, and deserving.
Building a life and body that reflect your truth, not someone else’s expectations.


From My Heart to Yours

If you’ve ever doubted your place, felt not good enough, or believed you had to prove your worth through perfection, I want you to hear this:

You are enough - right now, just as you are.
Your worth has never been tied to your achievements, appearance, or the opinions of others.

What truly matters is showing up for yourself each day, choosing progress over perfection - even when it isn't always visible, and remembering that the journey itself often holds more beauty and growth than the destination or reward could.

You are who you are when no one’s watching, and that truth will always matter more than external validation.

Movement saved me. It healed me. And I believe it can open doors for you too.

I’m here to walk beside you, every step of the way! ♡

🌸 A Note Before You Read

The stories I share here are to bring awareness to the intergenerational patterns many of us inherit - the unspoken wounds, expectations, and cultural norms that quietly shape who we become.

Every person in my story is shaped by their own environment - by unhealed pain, conditioning, and the patterns they learned to survive. My intention is to shed light on the cycles that shaped us all, not to assign blame to anyone. I see them as part of a much larger story - one that spans generations.

These reflections are about understanding - not accusing - and about healing together. Because when we choose awareness over silence, compassion over resentment, we begin to break the cycle for everyone who comes after us.

This story isn’t mine alone. It’s for anyone who’s ever dimmed their light to ease others' discomfort.
May it remind you to pause, to reflect, and to help end the patterns that once silenced you. ❤️
