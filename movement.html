Importance of Movement

The Importance of Movement

Movement isn’t just something you do — it’s how your body whispers, “I’m still here.”

Every stretch, every breath, every quiet sway is your body’s way of saying, “Thank you for noticing me.” This isn’t a call to push harder. It’s an invitation to move with awareness and honour your rhythm.

Find your rhythm →

Why movement matters

We often think of movement as a task. In truth, it’s a relationship — a way of partnering with your body.

  • Body: movement nourishes strength, mobility, and longevity • see note 1
  • Mind: it elevates mood and eases stress — a natural support for mental health • note 2
  • Heart: rhythmic movement helps regulate the nervous system and emotions • note 3
  • Soul: mindful motion deepens interoceptive awareness — feeling your inner world • note 4

“When you move with intention, your body becomes both the healer and the healed.”

Your relationship with movement

For years, I moved to change how I looked — chasing numbers and mirrors. Now, I move to remember how I feel.

Your body doesn’t need punishment. It needs partnership. When you move with your body, even the smallest motion becomes sacred.

What kinds of movement help?

There’s no one right way — only the way that brings you closer to yourself. A blend of strength, aerobic work, and mobility often supports the best outcomes note 5.

Strength

For resilience — a reminder you can hold your own weight.

Examples: squats, pushups, rows, carries

Cardio

For release — let your heart speak through rhythm and breath.

Examples: walking, dancing, cycling, swimming

Mobility & Flexibility

For restoration — create space where tension once lived.

Examples: hips, thoracic, ankles, hamstrings

Mindful Movement

For reflection — move not for progress, but for peace.

Examples: breathwork, yoga, tai chi, stillness

Balance body and mind

True balance isn’t found in perfect routines — it’s found in presence. Some days your body craves movement; other days it needs stillness. Both are sacred.

Before you move, ask: “What do I truly need right now — release, grounding, stillness, or expression?”

    Incorporate movement into a busy workday

    Movement happens in micro-moments. Small breaks improve focus and well-being note 6.

    Morning reset (2–3 min)

    Before opening your phone: gentle spinal rolls, neck circles, 10 slow breaths.

    Hourly micro-break

    Stand up for 60–90s, open chest, 10 deep breaths. Light movement every 30–60 min supports glucose control and energy note 6.

    Walking calls

    Take phone calls while walking — it boosts creativity and cognitive flexibility note 7.

    Desk mobility

    Wrists, thoracic opens, gentle rotations — better posture, less fatigue note 8.

    Stair or hallway laps

    2–5 minutes between tasks. Add one extra minute if a meeting overran.

    Evening unwind

    10-minute walk, easy yoga, or breathwork to shift from doing → resting.

    Movement as medicine

    Movement is how the body remembers joy. Even on slow or stiff days, it’s a quiet love letter to life.

    Start where you are. Move how your body wants to move. Trust that each breath, stretch, and heartbeat is part of your healing.

    You don’t have to move perfectly. You just have to move with presence.

    Notes & gentle references

    1. Longevity & daily movement: Meta-analyses show even modest daily activity is linked with reduced all-cause mortality (large cohort data).
    2. Mood & depression: Regular physical activity supports mood via endorphins/serotonin and stress-hormone modulation (medical school reviews & clinical trials).
    3. Emotion regulation: Rhythmic aerobic activity and dance can support parasympathetic balance and affect regulation.
    4. Interoception: Mindful movement (yoga/tai chi) improves body-awareness measures in controlled studies.
    5. Best outcomes: Blended programs (strength + aerobic + mobility) consistently outperform single-modality on multiple metrics.
    6. Micro-breaks: Light activity (2–3 min) every 30–60 min helps post-prandial glucose and subjective energy during sedentary work.
    7. Walking & creativity: Controlled experiments show walking increases creative output vs. seated work.
    8. Ergonomics: Desk-based mobility reduces musculoskeletal discomfort and fatigue in office settings.

    The stories I share here are to bring awareness to the intergenerational patterns many of us inherit - the unspoken wounds, expectations, and cultural norms that quietly shape who we become.

    Every person in my story (myself included) is shaped by their own environment - by unhealed pain, conditioning, and the patterns they learned to survive. My intention is to shed light on the cycles that shaped us all, not to assign blame to anyone. I see them as part of a much larger story - one that spans generations.

    These reflections are about understanding - not accusing - and about healing together. Because when we choose awareness over silence, compassion over resentment, we begin to break the cycle for everyone who comes after us.

    This story isn’t mine alone. It’s for anyone who’s ever dimmed their light to ease others' discomfort.
    May it remind you to pause, to reflect, and to help end the patterns that once silenced you. ❤️
