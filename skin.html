Unelearning Colorism



One of the very first things said about me came from my grandfather.
As soon as I was born, he looked at my mum and sighed, “Oh, this one is dark”, his voice heavy with disappointment - as though my existence was a flaw before I had even taken my first breath.

Heartbreakingly, this isn’t an uncommon story. Across South Asia, many girls born with a darker complexion are treated not as a blessing of life, but as a curse.



The Weight We Inherit

My mum carried the scars of colourism her whole life.
She was judged endlessly for the shade of her skin. My dad’s relatives would say cruel things to her - even warning her not to carry children, afraid her complexion would “transfer” to them.

She never truly healed from those wounds. And so, even as I grew up, she remained caught in the belief that her skin and her hair texture were flaws that needed fixing. Without meaning to, she passed that pain on to me.

I still remember overhearing her tell my dad one day, “Why didn’t you pass your fair genes to the kids?” as if our very existence had come out wrong. Those words carved deep. They became part of the lens through which I learned to see myself.

The Media’s Whisper

Everywhere I looked, the same message echoed. On TV, Fair & Lovely commercials showed a girl with dark skin magically transform into someone pale, confident, and suddenly “worthy.” The message was clear: fair = beautiful.

In movies, lighter skin was almost always cast as heroic. Darker skin was pushed into the shadows - villains, sidekicks, and comic relief. A quiet lesson repeated: dark was something to laugh at, fear, or dismiss, but never to admire. And just to show how deeply ingrained this bias was, some actresses even went as far as undergoing dangerous “fairness injections” just to climb higher in the industry.

At school, the pattern repeated. Fair-skinned girls were praised and chosen. If you were mixed-race, you were admired. If you were a white foreigner, you were practically glorified. Teachers favoured you, classmates gravitated toward you, opportunities came easier - and with every nod of approval, confidence bloomed.
For the rest of us, it meant fighting twice as hard for half the recognition.

Living in Fear of the Sun

These experiences left their mark on me. I grew up fearing the sun - not for my health, but for my complexion. Long sleeves at the beach. Umbrellas on bright days. Constantly hiding.

Being born with curls and a tan complexion in Sri Lanka felt like going to war with myself every single day - a battle that most girls like myself could painfully relate to.

Leaving Changed Everything

When I moved to Australia in 2019, the walls of that conditioning began to crack. Away from the constant chorus of fair = better, I began to see myself differently.

Strangers stopped me to compliment my hair and my skin tone - the very features I had despised all my life. It was confusing at first, but it planted a seed: maybe the problem was never me. Maybe it was the lens I’d been taught to see myself through.

The Truth About Colourism

That realisation pushed me to dig deeper - the historical roots of colorism. Sri Lanka wasn’t colonised once, but three times - by the Portuguese, the Dutch, and the British. With each wave came not only political rule, but cultural distortion.

They didn’t just govern us - they rewrote us. Our beauty standards, pride, and sense of identity were stripped away and replaced. Slowly, we were taught to believe their culture was ours. That fairness meant power. That whiteness meant beauty. That foreignness was superior.

But the truth is, even before colonisation, skin tone was already tied to privilege. Class and caste systems often associated lighter skin with status and indoor life, while darker skin was linked to labour under the sun. Colonial rule magnified this bias and cemented it into society. Fairer or mixed-race people were given preference in education, jobs, and social standing, while darker skin was pushed further into shame. Generations grew up absorbing this hierarchy until it became invisible, almost unquestioned.

And we absorbed it. We bleached our skin. We straightened our hair. We chased lighter features as though they were tickets to acceptance, love, and status. In the process, we forgot that our skin - rich with melanin - was made to thrive under our sun. It was never a curse, but a kind of armour.

Coming Back to Myself

Learning this history didn’t just educate me - it freed me. I realised the shame was never mine to carry.

I stopped hiding under umbrellas. I let my skin meet the sun again. And something incredible happened: my skin, once ashy and dull from being hidden away, began to glow. It turned golden, radiant, alive.

I stopped chasing a lighter version of myself. And I stopped seeing my mum’s features in me as a curse. I now see them as gifts - ones she couldn’t embrace, but I can.

To Every Brown Girl Reading This

If you’ve ever been called “too dark.” If you’ve ever wished to look lighter just to feel worthy. If you’ve ever felt invisible in your own skin - please know this: you were never the problem. The system was. The conditioning was. The lies were.

You don’t have to carry them anymore. You can put them down. You can reclaim yourself. Your skin is not a burden. It is a blessing. Every shade of brown carries history, resilience, and beauty the world once tried to erase.


Breaking the Cycle Together

I couldn’t heal my mum’s wounds for her. But I can break the cycle for myself - and for the generations to come.

No more shrinking. No more bleaching. No more hiding. We are the generation that rises and says: enough. The generation that will rewrite the story for our daughters.

To them we will say:
Brown is radiant. Dark is fierce. And every shade in between is sacred - worthy of love, honour, and pride.


A Message to You

You were never the flaw - the standard was.

Claim your skin. Wear it like armour. Walk in it like royalty.

✨ This skin - our skin - was never something to erase. It is the soil of our ancestors, the glow of the sun, the strength of generations.

Let them measure beauty in borrowed scales - we will shine anyway.

Lesson for life: your worth is not up for bleaching. Honour your roots, honour your shade, and never again apologise for the fire you carry in the colour you were born to be. ♡
